### What this tells us about Dentour's spec discipline

Dentour's spec corpus is **two distinct populations stacked together**: seven stable capabilities (`auth`, `multi-domain-routing`, `multi-currency`, `patient-onboarding`, `clinic-onboarding`, `quote-workflow`, `clinic-profile`) that carry full `### Requirement:` / `#### Scenario:` bodies with `## Implementation map` pointers, and five Phase-0 skeletons (`patient-account`, `clinic-portal`, `admin`, `dental-chart`, `messaging`) that are explicit placeholders for Phase 1 retrospective baseline authoring. This is **acknowledged uneven coverage**, not unmanaged drift — Dentour's own `openspec/INDEX.md` classifies these specs as a "Wave C" backlog item.

Where the stable specs land, the discipline is **noticeably honest about its own gaps**. The `clinic-onboarding` spec documents the CSRF skip on `/api/v1/clinic-onboarding/*` as a REQ rather than hiding it; the `auth` spec explicitly carries D3 (no CSRF at large) and D7 (CORS pattern) as known-gap entries; the `admin` skeleton names C1 (no Sentry) up-front; the `multi-domain-routing` spec notes the admin filter UI lag. This is the spec convention working as intended — it gives the studio a defensible read on which seams are known to Dentour (most of them) versus which are likely surprises (very few). The drift audit's stable-spec rows are therefore mostly low-severity: where the code differs from a hypothetical "ideal" implementation, the spec is usually already aware.

The four MEDIUM-severity findings cluster around two distinct themes worth surfacing at kickoff:

1. **Governance-vs-reality drifts that the per-capability specs do not directly own** (#2 multi-domain-routing six locales vs four documented; #3 multi-currency stale-rate-fallback date of `2026-03-10`). These are not per-capability bugs; they are calibration gaps between CLAUDE.md / `openspec/config.yaml` / `.github/copilot-instructions.md` and the actual deployed surface. The convention adapter (Phase 6 of `onboard-dentour`) needs to know which document wins when they disagree.
2. **Phase-0 skeletons with high-stakes code surface** (#9 clinic-portal, #11 dental-chart). The `clinic-portal` app is a full Cloudflare-Pages-deployed Next.js application with a role / permission model that protects per-clinic data, yet has zero documented requirements at HEAD; the `dental-chart` is a clinical-domain primitive consumed by both patient display and clinic editing flows but its tooth-numbering convention is unspecified. Both are explicit Phase-0 → Phase-1 backlog items in Dentour's own classification, but the audit flags them at medium because the *risk-weighted gap* (large code surface × no spec) is higher than the average skeleton.

Crucially, **zero rows are scored HIGH**. This is by design and stated in the audit-strategy frontmatter: the audit's observed-behavior layer is the Phase 1 topology scout, not direct source-level reads, so the severity ceiling is medium for anything that depends on code-level inspection. A future Phase-2-bis pass with direct file reads against the `dentour-org/dentour` checkout could promote some of the medium rows to high (most likely candidates: the multi-currency stale-rate logic, the clinic-portal role / permission model) if specific contradictions surface — but that pass is out of scope for this change.

Three cross-cutting drifts surfaced by Phase 1 but **not owned by any single capability** are recorded here for downstream-phase reference, not as audit rows:

- **Patient-web is deployed via Cloudflare Pages static export, not Cloudflare Workers SSR** (topology § 9 #1). CLAUDE.md / `openspec/config.yaml` / `.github/copilot-instructions.md` all describe the SSR path; reality at HEAD is static export. The Workers SSR path is wired but staging-only. None of the 12 capability specs ranges over deployment topology directly, so this drift lives in the cross-cutting layer rather than in any one capability row.
- **`pnpm openspec:validate` is not in CI** (topology § 9 #4). This contradicts `onboard-dentour/design.md § Why Dentour is not a copy-paste of Unbind` item 2, which asserts "Dentour's CI requires it to pass". Phase 6 of `onboard-dentour` (convention adapter design) needs to absorb this — wiring validate into CI becomes a possible studio contribution, not a constraint to satisfy.
- **`@dentour/api-client` is generated but consumed by no app** (topology § 9 #12). Each frontend ships its own `lib/api/*` fetch wrapper. Any spec change that touches "the API client" must be explicit about whether it lands in the generated package or in each per-app wrapper. This affects every capability row that touches the request path (#1, #2, #4, #5, #6, #7) but is not severe enough at any single spec level to score above low.

**Implication for the studio's convention adapter (Phase 6 of `onboard-dentour`):** Dentour's stable specs are reliable enough to be treated as authoritative source-of-truth for "what should be there" — comparable to Vivod's, with a similar self-aware tone about gaps. The five Phase-0 skeletons are *not* reliable contracts at HEAD; the adapter should refuse to plan substantive work against a skeleton spec until Phase 1 retrospective authoring lands on Dentour's side. The natural first Forge run on Dentour is therefore **either** the `messaging` super-session (already flagged for replacement by AI Communication Layer in spec) **or** a Phase-1-style retrospective baseline authoring change against one of the five skeletons — both are studio-shaped problems (specification-as-deliverable) rather than code-shaped problems, which aligns with the proposed `hybrid` engagement mode.

## Sharing decision

**Studio-internal by default.** Per Phase 2 T2.3 and the design.md § Drift audit scope, the primary purpose of this audit is to give the studio a defensible position at the kickoff conversation — "we read your specs, we read your topology, here are the seams we noticed" — not to grade Dentour's existing discipline. The drift-audit's honesty about its own depth limit (topology-anchored, not source-anchored) makes it shareable as-is without overclaiming.

Sharing with Dentour at kickoff is a strategic call (TBD). Two considerations make the calculus slightly different from Vivod:

1. **Five Phase-0 skeletons are a public Dentour-side acknowledgement of incomplete coverage.** The audit's findings on those rows are essentially restating Dentour's own roadmap ("Wave C will document these"); sharing them is not new information to Dentour, but it does demonstrate that the studio has read the spec corpus in full and understands the implied prioritisation. Low cost, modest signal.
2. **The medium-severity governance-vs-reality drifts (#2, #3) are not in any Dentour spec.** They are cross-document drifts between `openspec/specs/*/spec.md` and CLAUDE.md / `openspec/config.yaml`. Sharing these would be the studio surfacing drifts Dentour has not yet noticed — higher signal but also higher delicacy. The natural framing is "we found these and would like to confirm at kickoff which document wins" rather than "we found bugs in your documentation".

The natural moment to share is **after charter signing, before the first Forge run**, alongside the convention-adapter design (Phase 6 deliverable). At that point the audit serves as a calibration anchor for how aggressively the studio will rely on Dentour's specs to plan work — particularly for the choice of which capability to target first (messaging super-session vs skeleton retrospective).

Frontmatter carries `shareability: studio-internal-default` until that call is made.
